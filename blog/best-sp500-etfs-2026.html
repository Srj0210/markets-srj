Best S&P 500 ETFs to Buy in 2026: SPY vs VOO vs IVV

Updated April 2026 · 8 min read

The S&P 500 index is the benchmark of the US stock market, tracking 500 of America's largest public companies. For most investors, buying an S&P 500 ETF is the simplest and most effective way to invest in the broad market.

But with multiple S&P 500 ETFs available, which one should you choose? In this article, we compare the three most popular options: SPY, VOO, and IVV.

1. SPDR S&P 500 ETF Trust (SPY)

SPY is the oldest and most heavily traded ETF in the world, launched in 1993. It's managed by State Street Global Advisors and has become the gold standard for S&P 500 investing.

Expense Ratio: 0.0945% — the highest among the three, but still very low. For every $10,000 invested, you'd pay about $9.45 per year in fees.

Best For: Active traders who need high liquidity and tight bid-ask spreads. SPY trades more volume than any other ETF, making it ideal for options trading and day trading.

2. Vanguard S&P 500 ETF (VOO)

VOO is Vanguard's flagship S&P 500 ETF, launched in 2010. Vanguard is known for its investor-friendly approach and low costs.

Expense Ratio: 0.03% — one of the lowest in the industry. For $10,000 invested, you'd pay just $3 per year.

Best For: Long-term buy-and-hold investors. VOO's rock-bottom expense ratio means more of your money stays invested and compounding over time.

3. iShares Core S&P 500 ETF (IVV)

IVV is BlackRock's S&P 500 ETF, launched in 2000. BlackRock is the world's largest asset manager through its iShares brand.

Expense Ratio: 0.03% — tied with VOO as the cheapest option.

Best For: Investors who already use the iShares ecosystem or whose brokerage offers commission-free iShares trading.

Comparison Table

SPY — Expense: 0.0945%, AUM: ~$550B, Launched: 1993, Best for: Traders
VOO — Expense: 0.03%, AUM: ~$400B, Launched: 2010, Best for: Long-term investors
IVV — Expense: 0.03%, AUM: ~$450B, Launched: 2000, Best for: iShares users

Which One Should You Buy?

For most long-term investors, VOO or IVV are the better choice due to their lower expense ratio. The 0.06% difference from SPY may seem tiny, but over 30 years on a $100,000 investment, it adds up to thousands of dollars.

If you're an active trader or need options liquidity, SPY is the clear winner with its massive daily trading volume.

The good news? All three ETFs track the exact same index, so their long-term returns are virtually identical. You can't go wrong with any of them.
